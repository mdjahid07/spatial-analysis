FRACTAL DIMENSION ANALYSIS
Understanding Fractal Dimension Analysis (FDA)

Fractal Dimension Analysis is a mathematical method used to quantify the complexity and spatial distribution of urban forms. The fractal dimension (D) value ranges between 1 and 2 for 2-dimensional spaces, with values closer to 2 indicating more complex, space-filling structures.

Interpreting the Map

High Fractal Dimension Areas (D close to 2): These areas are highly complex and densely developed. They might indicate regions with intricate street patterns, mixed land use, and dense building arrangements. Implications: Likely to be central business districts or highly urbanized zones with significant commercial and residential activity. Medium Fractal Dimension Areas (D between 1.5 and 1.9): These areas show moderate complexity. They might have a mix of urban and suburban characteristics, with some variation in building density and street patterns. Implications: Could be transitional zones between the urban core and suburban/rural areas, possibly with mixed development and varying land uses. Low Fractal Dimension Areas (D close to 1): These areas have simpler, less dense patterns. They could represent suburban or peri-urban regions with larger plots of land and fewer buildings. Implications: Likely to be residential areas with lower density, open spaces, or regions at the urban fringe.

LOCAL INDICATORS OF SPATIAL ASSOCIATION (LISA)

LISA analysis is used to identify spatial clusters and spatial outliers in a dataset. It typically examines spatial autocorrelation, indicating whether similar values cluster together (positive spatial autocorrelation) or whether dissimilar values are adjacent (negative spatial autocorrelation).
I recently completed a spatial analysis project for Basirhat Block 1, focusing on urban patches using LISA clusters to uncover significant spatial patterns. The LISA analysis, conducted using GeoDa software and visualized in ArcGIS, provides insights into the spatial distribution of urban development.

Here's what the map reveals:

High-High Clusters: These areas represent significant clusters where high urbanization patches are surrounded by other high urbanization patches. This indicates a strong positive spatial autocorrelation, suggesting that urban development is concentrated in specific regions.
Low-Low Clusters: In contrast, these clusters highlight areas of low urbanization surrounded by other low urbanization patches, showing where development is sparse or non-existent.
High-Low and Low-High Outliers: These clusters indicate anomalies in the pattern, where a high urbanization patch is surrounded by low urbanization patches or vice versa. This could signal unique factors driving urban development in these areas.
